\section{Building the Toolkit}

% Talk about buildling that problem solving tooklit - then link to making notes? 

In this chapter, I would like to challenge the common misconception that a Maths degree is nothing more than learning more advanced techniques.

If there’s one thing I’d like you to remember while studying mathematics, it’s this: the things you learn didn’t appear out of nowhere.

Every definition, every theorem, every technique you come across exists because at some point someone was trying to solve a problem.

When you first encounter a new topic, it can sometimes feel like a wall of definitions followed by a list of results you’re expected to accept and apply. And it’s easy to walk away thinking that mathematics is just a collection of rules someone decided on a long time ago.

But that’s not really how it developed.

Mathematicians in the past were trying to understand patterns, structures, and problems that genuinely puzzled them. The definitions and theorems you see today are often the end result of a long process of people asking questions, trying ideas, getting stuck, and refining their thinking.

In other words, mathematics didn’t start with the definitions. It started with curiosity.

Once you start viewing mathematical ideas as tools that were developed to tackle specific problems, things begin to feel a little less arbitrary. A theorem stops being just a statement you’re supposed to memorise, and starts looking more like a clever idea someone came up with because it helped them get somewhere.

Of course, when you first learn a topic, you’re usually seeing the polished version, the final structure that survived years of refinement. The messy exploration that produced it often isn’t visible in a lecture course. 

That can make the subject feel colder than it really is. But behind every theorem there was once a mathematician trying to make sense of something that didn’t quite add up.

You might not immediately appreciate every idea in the same way they did, and that’s completely normal. Some motivations only become clearer much later, when you see how different areas connect.

Still, it’s worth remembering that the mathematics you’re learning exists because someone, somewhere, cared enough about a problem to keep thinking about it.

And in a way, that’s what you’re joining when you study it. What this degree really trains is how you respond when you don't immediately know what to you. 

At school, most problems you might've dealt with followed a recognisable pattern. You learned a method, applied it and improved with practice. At university, you will encounter questions that don't look like anything you've seen before. And that's deliberate. 

Mathematics is not training you to memorise procedures, but training you to sit with uncertainty without panicking, to break a large problem into smaller parts and identify structures where none seems obvious in the first glance.

When faced with something unfamiliar, mathematics teaches you to ask: 
\begin{itemize}
    \item What do I actually know? 
    \item What is the definition here? 
    \item What would have to be true for this to work? 
    \item Can I test a simpler example with numbers before generalising it? 
\end{itemize}

Those questions become automatic over time.

There seems to be a common myth that good notes always equal good understanding. Spoiler: they don't. 

Note taking is not about transcription, but about processing. You might even spend the first few weeks trying to find the `right' way to take notes for something like Mathematics. 

Some people swear by handwriting because it slows them down, others type in \LaTeX \ or something else because it might be neater and easier to organise, some annotate the lecture slides or notes directly.  

There is no `one-size-fits-all' here. What works brilliantly for one person might be useless for another. The only reliable test is whether the system helps you think critically or not. 

If you find yourself with beautiful notes on the most recent Calculus lecture you attended but have no idea on how to approach the problem sheet, something needs to change. If you find not making any notes in lectures and reviewing information later in the evening helps ideas settle, try that. Or if you realise handwriting slows you down, see if you can find a way to condense information instead of copying a lot of things down. 

University requires experimentation, not perfection. The goal here is not to produce the most elegant looking notes amongst your peers, but to build understanding that you can use for yourself. You are not being graded on your notes, but on your thinking, so ensure that the learning resources you use/build contribute to your thinking. 

\subsection{Explaining Things Out Loud}
% cite/reference the 'feynman' technique in the margin or something?

There is one habit that has helped me more than any particular note-taking method, and it has nothing to do with whether I typed or wrote by hand. It was explaining things out loud. 

A good test to see if you've understood something is usually later in the day or in the week, close your notes and try to explain the main idea either to a friend or out loud. 

You very quickly discover what you don't understand when you try to speak it. On paper, everything can look fine. But the moment you try to explain why some assumption is necessary, or why an implication follows, you feel the gap immediately. And that gap is useful information, it tells you exactly where to focus. 

If you can explain a concept to someone else, even to an imaginary audience without constantly checking your notes, you are much closer to understanding a concept than if you simply reproduce what was written in the lecture. 

Mathematics is not about producing the most beautiful notes, it's about producing clarity and understanding of the concepts learned. 

\section{What to Do When You're Stuck}

Problem solving sounds very inspiring until you're staring at Question 4 from Coursework 2 at 10:37pm or 38 minutes into the exam and your brain has gone offline. What I mean to say is, there will be moments in lectures or problem sheets or exams where you may genuinely not know how to begin. 

The instinct when stuck is often to rush, to scan the question faster or hope something jumps out. 

Instead, try and slow down. Read the question again. What is actually being asked? Is it existence? Bijectivity? Optimsation? 

Rewrite the question in simpler terms and alongside it, any definitions or theorem you can recall about what is being asked. 

This does two things. First, it forces your brain back into structure and something you do know rather than panic. And secondly, everything that you need is given to you in the words of the question, and once you have unpicked the most important parts, you have part of a path forward. 

Often when you're trying to prove something, or show something holds, you want to look at the statement and ask yourself, `what would have to be true for this to work?` and work backwards from there. Working backwards from what you know is true can help you get started. 

Over time, you might start noticing that the moments of being stuck becomes less frightening. Not because you always know what to do, but because you trust that you have strategies. And that trust is part of the toolkit.  

\section{On Gaining Insight}
% Need example of when I was really stuck on stuff 
\begin{quote}
One of the hardest things to unlearn at university is the urge to look at a solution the moment you feel stuck. Once you've seen someone else's solution, you can never recreate the benefit of discovering it yourself. Even if you don't solve the problem completely, struggling with it, forming wrong ideas, testing different approaches and hitting multiple dead ends does far more for your mathematical growth than neatly following a polished answer ever will.
\end{quote}

To illustrate this with an example, there was a problem in my Linear Algebra II module where I was stuck on what, in hindsight, is a very simple result: show that the set $V^S$ of all maps form a set $S$ to a vector space $V$ was a vector space. This sentence probably means very little unless you have already studied Linear Algebra, and that's fine. The technical details don't matter here. 

What matters is what the problem requires. To show something is a vector space, we just need to check the definition we are given with the axioms and see if they are consistent. For this question, we only had to check that there exists an additive identity, every element has an additive inverse, and that one of the distributivity law holds.

When I first read the question, I understood the definition of an additive identity in theory. I knew what a $0_V$ meant in vector space, but I didn't know how to write the proof properly in this new setting, I was struggling with turning the idea in my head into a clean mathematically sound argument on paper. 

Now, I could've easily looked up the proof or asked AI to generate it for me in a few seconds, and it would have probably told me to let $0(s) = 0_V , \ \forall s \in S$ then check $(f+0)(s) = f(s)$. It would have been correct, looked neat, and I could have convinced myself I understood it. 

But I didn't. Instead, I was in the workshop where I had my lecturer from my Linear Algebra II module present to talk me through the problem and refine the gaps in my thinking. 

Instead of being simply told what I had to write for the proof to fall out, we started from what I did know about the definition of additive identity and what it was supposed to satisfy, namely the condition $f +0 =f$, then treated this like an equation and worked out what this mysterious `zero function' would look like. 

So that day I learned that the zero element in that space of functions is just the function that sends every input to the zero vector. But arriving at that idea, and more importantly, learning how to justify it properly, was the real insight.  

What clicked in that workshop moment wasn't just the answer to one problem but how proofs in algebra work. You start from definitions, think about what must be true, and sometimes, work backwards to get a full picture of the proof before starting to write it. 

The point being, that insight didn't come from seeing the finished proof. It came from wrestling with the gap between knowing the definition and knowing how to write it in the abstract way the questions asked me to. Now, if I had skipped that part and just read up the solution, I would have `known' the answer, but would have lost the experience of learning how to reconstruct that kind of proof myself. 

And that's the difference. 

When you outsource your thinking, you might take pride in being incredibly efficient. But the price of efficiency is ownership, and the price of speed is insight, and with a subject as elegant and satisfying like Mathematics, that trade is just not worth it. 

\section{Why This Matters}

This reason these problem solving skills matter is not because you will be constantly proving theorems outside university. 

It matters because the habits transfer.

Learning how to decompose complex problems into manageable parts, staying calm under uncertainty, testing assumptions, communicating reasoning clearly. These are all thinking skills. A maths degree does not just teach content, but reshapes how you approach problems in general.
